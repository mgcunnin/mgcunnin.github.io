Rearranging the World

A Pragmatist Reading of a Nineteenth-Century River Map

Myles Cunningham

At first, Map of the Principal Rivers Showing Their Courses, Countries, and Comparative Lengths is striking as a purely visual object. It depicts a set of rivers radiating outward from an imaginary central sea. Looking more closely, the map becomes stranger: these are real rivers from around the world juxtaposed into an impossible geography. Each river retains a recognizable course and fragments of its geographic surroundings, but the way that they have been assembled and arranged has been invented. It immediately challenges our assumption that features adjacent on a map are also adjacent in the terrain that map represents. Yet it also would not be correct to describe it as inaccurate. he peculiar arrangement is the point. The map participates in a cartographic project of comparison, aggregation, and ultimately the production of knowledge. This map demonstrates that maps are not merely containers for information but also organize the relationships that make that information meaningful.

An Artificial Geography

The map was engraved by Thomas Starling and published by Baldwin and Cradock in 1834, under the direction of the Society for the Diffusion of Useful Knowledge, an organization which produced inexpensive informational materials explicitly intended to expand popular access to education. The map1 presents more than 70 river courses, each numbered and keyed to an index. The rivers are arranged so that their orientation roughly corresponds to compass direction (the north-flowing Nile, for example, runs upward from the bottom of the page). Each river retains a few selected contextual details, including cities, lakes, region names, and hachured topography. Different line styles are used to indicate each river’s continent of origin. Concentric circles radiating from the center indicate approximate direct distance from the sea, while the index separately records the greater length of each river when its major bends are followed. Two smaller clusters near the top of the page display an additional handful of rivers.

The map’s unusual design offers many avenues for analysis, from questions of representation and accuracy to issues of how geographic knowledge is organized and authorized. It is especially open to readings that fall broadly within the critical cartographic tradition, which begins from the recognition that maps are not neutral windows onto the world. Not only do they necessarily select, classify, simplify, and organize their subjects, but the ways that they do so are shaped by power relations.2 This map’s selections and exclusions, as well as the authority conveyed by its visual order, invite questions about what counted as geographic knowledge, how that knowledge was organized, and how it circulated through the imperial and pedagogical institutions of the nineteenth century.

The pragmatist philosophical tradition, and particularly the work of John Dewey, offers a way to extend this critique. Dewey’s pragmatism treats selection and classification as practical operations: ways of transforming the world so that particular relationships can be perceived and put to use. In this process, some questions become easier to ask, and some features become easier to notice while others are pushed from view. A pragmatist reading shifts the analysis from what the map represents to what it does. How does the artificial geography of this map reorganize our experience, direct attention, form habits of perception, and ultimately help to construct a particular form of knowledge?6

A Pragmatist Way of Reading Maps

Pragmatism is sometimes simplified to the view that something is true if it “works.” Dewey’s version is more precise: ideas are tested by how well they help us understand and resolve problems. They are also evaluated by whether their results hold up under further experience. He described this process as inquiry.3 Although the term is used here in a technical sense, it refers to something familiar: the effort to turn an uncertain situation into one that is more settled and intelligible. Things like ideas, writing, and maps do not merely record conclusions, but take an active part in this process. When they do so, they help determine what we can observe, what we can conceptualize, and ultimately, what we can do.4

This is why Dewey could call maps “propositions.”5 He did not mean that maps make verbal claims in the way sentences do. Rather, a map selects features, arranges them in relation to one another, and presents them in a form that can guide further observation and thought. A map is therefore not simply a mirror of nature, but an instrument for organizing a problem. Seen this way, the map of rivers reorganizes geographic information so that readers can examine it differently. It allows them to trace and test patterns that would be difficult to perceive on a conventional map.

A pragmatist reading of this map raises three questions: What does the map enable a reader to do? What must it rearrange or simplify to make that possible? And what habits of attention does it encourage?

What Does the Map Make Possible?

On the surface of the earth, or on an ordinary map, these rivers do not appear together, ready for comparison. This map must create the conditions that make such comparison possible. It does so by removing the rivers from their geographic settings, selecting which features to retain, and placing them within a shared visual system.

Rather than asking what a map represents, a pragmatist reading would ask what its arrangement enables a reader to do. Here, readers can trace courses, compare rivers’ reach and length, and, with the aid of the index, move between visual and numerical information. By bringing rivers from widely separated places into one visual field, the map helps to constitute “the world’s principal rivers” as a coherent and comparable category.

This reorganization is easy to overlook in a more conventional map, because cartographic conventions have become familiar to us and come to appear natural. The strangeness of this map makes it easier to see this process. We can recognize that it has been built, intentionally or not, for a particular purpose. In Dewey’s terms, it is the framework for a project of inquiry.

The Production of Useful Knowledge

The map is a part of the large educational project of the Society for the Diffusion of Useful Knowledge, an organization associated with leading members of the British Whig party. The Society sought to widen access to scientific and geographic information through inexpensive publications, especially for the benefit of readers who lacked access to formal education or expensive books. 9 Though this ambition was democratic, offering wider access did not mean upending intellectual authority. The Society’s editors and expert contributors still decided what counted as “useful” knowledge, which categories would organize it, and, in turn, what version of the world readers would encounter.

This map was not directly a tool of colonial administration. Even so, it belonged to an imperial-era system that gathered observations from around the world and standardized them within British scientific and educational conventions. The map’s biases lie not only in the representations that appear on the page, but also in the way that that institutions like the SDUK selected and circulated these representations as authoritative knowledge. In this way, they help to determine the frameworks through which their readers encountered both conceptual categories like “rivers” as well as different regions of the world.

This authority becomes clearer when the map is placed beside other ways of representing the same subject. This map belongs to a broader nineteenth-century tradition of comparative charts that assembled geographically dispersed features like rivers or mountains into a single visual field.10 A more representative example is the 1822 Map of the Principal Rivers throughout the World, published by H. C. Carey and I. Lea. This chart simplifies rivers into elongated lines, with only minor squiggles as nods to their courses. In contrast, a conventional geographic map does nearly the opposite: it preserves relative location, adjacency, and regional context, while making direct comparison among widely dispersed features difficult.

Rearranging the World

This map’s impossible geography makes visible what more familiar maps allow us to forget: geographic knowledge is shaped by the forms through which maps present the world. A pragmatist reading shows that this rearrangement is not merely distortion, but an operation that opens some possibilities while limiting others. Map of the Principal Rivers is especially revealing because it displays that process so openly. Its conspicuous choices help us see how cartographic form can turn historically situated judgments into authoritative ways of seeing and experiencing the world.
